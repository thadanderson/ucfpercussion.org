_Contributed by Tara McGurk, class of 2023_

Congratulations! You have earned the Principal role for your respective large ensemble. With this role, there comes many responsibilities and logistics that need to happen to ensure a smooth semester. This guideline was designed to answer any questions you may have as well as outline your duties as the **Principal Percussionist**.
### Communication within your ensemble
The first step after the large ensemble audition is to make a group chat in Teams of all of the percussionists within your ensemble. This will serve as the main source of communication for part assignments, rehearsal and concert schedules, equipment logistics, etc.
### Part Assignments
As the principal percussionist, it is your job to assign and distribute parts to the percussionists within your ensemble. The first day of the semester, you should arrange to meet with the director of your large ensemble in their office to introduce yourself and obtain the music for the semester.

When it comes to assigning the parts, it is up to your discretion whether you wish to assign the parts yourself, or let the members of your ensemble weigh in on what they would like to play. Part assignments should be done as soon as you get the music to ensure preparedness for the first rehearsal.

[Part Assignments Template coming soon]

Once you have made the part assignments for every piece on the current concert cycle, send the part assignments to the Teams group chat, and arrange to meet with the members of your ensemble to distribute the parts if they are not digitally available.
### Rehearsals
Typically the director of your large ensemble will either post a rehearsal schedule on the bulletin board upstairs, or send it out via WebCourses. It is always a good idea to take a picture or screenshot of the schedule and send it to your Microsoft Teams group chat as an additional reminder of the schedule in an easy-to-access place.

It is standard to arrive to rehearsals at least 20-minutes before the start of the rehearsal to allow for proper set up time.
### Equipment
Most equipment needed for large ensembles is kept in the Rehearsal Hall. However, there are some instances where certain pieces call for smaller pieces of equipment that is kept in the Performing Arts Center.

When transporting equipment too and from the Rehearsal Hall, it is your responsibility to post about it in the Teams “Equipment” channel to ensure that no one needs that particular instrument during rehearsal time. In some instances, you will be able to keep the equipment you need at the Rehearsal Hall for the duration of the concert cycle, with the approval of your professor and studio mates.
### Set Up Diagrams
Large ensemble concerts typically come with the demand of a large amount of percussion instruments. Because of this, it is a good idea to make a set up diagram for the instruments needed in each concert cycle. Look over all of the pieces that are being played during the concert cycle. Which instruments need to be close to each other? Which instruments are being shared between two different players? Do you need more than one of a certain toy to limit the amount of walking around? The goal with the percussion set up is to not have to move any instruments or implements during the concert.

Set up diagrams can be made on a tablet, computer, or just with paper and pen. Once you have made it, send it to your Microsoft Teams group chat. Having a set up diagram helps with efficiency when setting up for rehearsals so everyone knows where everything goes.

### Load Lists and Concerts
**AT LEAST ONE WEEK BEFORE A CONCERT OR PERFORMANCE,** it is your responsibility to get with your section to make a load list of the equipment and implements needed for a performance. This can either be done in person or via your Teams group chat. You will then organize it into the Load List Template that can be found here:

[Load List Template]

When making load lists, it is important to be detail oriented. You need to not only take into consideration small toys, cymbals, or drums, but also list the stands that they go on as well. It is also a good idea to keep track of larger implements that individuals might not necessarily own (i.e. bass drum beaters, chime mallets, tam tam beaters).

Once the load list is made, you need to communicate with your section as well as the other principal of the ensemble that you are sharing the concert with to choose a time to load or transport the equipment. This step also includes communicating with the ensemble directors as well, for information regarding when the room will be available for on-campus performances, or when the truck will arrive at the building for off-campus performances.

If it is an off campus performance, it is your job to have the load list to make sure everything gets out of their respective rooms and onto the truck. Also, off campus venues may have their own large instruments so it’s important to check with your ensemble director about contacting the host.
### Post-Concert Responsibilities
Immediately after the conclusion of the concert, if your ensemble used original copies, it is your job to collect all of the original copies and folders from your section and return them to your ensemble director the next time you see them. If the concert was held off-campus, it is important to stand by the truck with the load list to ensure that everything that was brought with you is also returning to campus. Once back on campus, every instrument must go back to its original place. This includes equipment that was at the Rehearsal Hall for the concert cycle but typically lives at the PAC.
